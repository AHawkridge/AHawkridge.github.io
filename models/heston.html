The Heston model proposed the concept of stochastic volatility, where the volatility itself is has its own separate stochastic differential equation. This concept was proposed first introduced in 1987 by J.Hull and A.White \cite{HULL1987}. Then E.Stein and J.Stein managed to generalise the Black-Scholes model to allow for stochastic volatility in 1991 \cite{stein1991stock}, and then further developed by S.Heston in 1993 \cite{heston1993closed} who the modelled is named after. These papers discuss the development of the stochastic differential equation of the volatility where there needs to be a correlation between the wiener process in the volatility SDE and the GBM, the correlation value that was determined was $p=0.6$, although in some later revisions this value can change. In the Heston model the volatility follows an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process: \[ dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma_t S_t dW_t^S \] \[ d\sigma_t = -\theta \sigma dt + \delta dW_t^\sigma \] Then through the use of Ito's lemma the variance ($v$) is given by: \[ dv_t = \kappa (\theta -v_t)dt + \xi \sigma dW_t^\sigma \] A multivariate normal distribution is used here , to ensure there is correlation between the Weiner processes. $\theta$ is the long term variance of the SDE, it is the value at which the variance is expected to tend towards as $t \rightarrow \infty$, $\kappa$ is the rate at which the variance tends to $\theta$ and lastly $\xi$ is the volatility of the volatility SDE which determines the variance. It can be seen that the Heston model uses the variance rather than the volatility, this is because the volatility can become negative in the above SDE and as such the value is squared to only deal with positive values.
